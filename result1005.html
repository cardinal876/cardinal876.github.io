Steel

By: James T. Example, University of Oregon

The article is intended for viewing the user interface and is not representative of a typical article on edwinbook. Steel, one of the most essential materials in the modern world, is an alloy primarily composed of iron and carbon. From the skyscrapers that define city skylines to the cars we drive and the tools we use, steel forms the backbone of industrial civilization. What makes steel extraordinary is not only its strength and durability but also its adaptability, making it the material of choice across centuries. From swords and bridges to rockets and skyscrapers, steel’s story is deeply intertwined with the progress of human society.

Steel is simply a form of iron that has been refined by controlling the amount of carbon within it. While pure iron is soft and malleable, the addition of carbon strengthens it significantly, allowing it to be shaped into materials that balance toughness and flexibility. Human use of steel dates back over two millennia, with early blacksmiths in places like India developing high-quality steels such as the famed “wootz” steel, prized across the ancient world for its remarkable sharpness and durability. Yet steel remained difficult and expensive to produce until breakthroughs in modern smelting techniques made it possible to manufacture in large quantities. The ability to produce steel efficiently was the turning point that transformed it from a luxury metal into the foundation of modern industry.

The production of steel took a significant leap forward in Europe during the 17th century with the advent of the blast furnace. These furnaces allowed for the efficient smelting of iron ore into pig iron, which could then be refined into steel. Yet it was the 19th century that truly revolutionized steelmaking, thanks to innovations like the Bessemer process, which dramatically lowered the cost of producing large quantities of steel.

The Rise of Steel and Its Role in the Industrial Revolution

As the world entered the 19th century, steel became the key material of the Industrial Revolution. The Bessemer process, developed in the 1850s, allowed molten pig iron to be converted into steel by blowing air through it to reduce impurities. This made steel production faster, cheaper, and scalable. Soon, railways, bridges, and factories were being constructed with steel, propelling economies forward and enabling entirely new forms of industry.

The expansion of steel production was vital in shaping the modern world. Railroads enabled the mass movement of goods and people across continents, steel ships crossed oceans with unmatched durability, and steel-framed skyscrapers reshaped urban landscapes. The famous Brooklyn Bridge, completed in 1883, relied on over 14,000 miles of steel wire to support its revolutionary suspension design, while the Eiffel Tower, completed in 1889, stood at a height of 984 feet, making it the tallest man-made structure on Earth at the time. A temporary structure completed for the 1889 World's Fair in Paris, it was left standing for radio transmission and has become one of the most recognizable landmarks. These projects were not only engineering marvels but also cultural icons, proof that the age of steel had arrived.

Steel’s role did not stop with railroads and bridges. It became crucial in military applications, from armor plating to weaponry, with the late 19th and early 20th centuries witnessing steel industries emerge as symbols of national power. Countries with large steel capacities, such as the United States, Germany, and Britain, rose as global industrial leaders, competing not only economically but also militarily.

The impact of steel during the Industrial Revolution was felt around the globe. Nations without strong steel industries were often left behind, while those that mastered its production surged ahead. This divide helped set the stage for global power dynamics that lasted well into the 20th century, where steel capacity was often equated with national strength.

The Story of Steel in the 20th and 21st Century

As the world moved into the 20th century, steel’s importance continued to expand. It was vital in the construction of automobiles, airplanes, and the massive infrastructure projects of the early 20th century. The United States became the world’s largest steel producer by the early 1900s, powering both its domestic growth and international influence.

World War I and World War II further emphasized steel’s importance. Tanks, battleships, and aircraft all relied on steel, and the nations that could produce it at scale often had significant advantages on the battlefield. Following the wars, steel production surged as countries rebuilt their economies and cities, laying the foundations for modern infrastructure.

The 20th century also saw the rise of new steel-producing countries. Japan, South Korea, and later China emerged as major players. By the late 20th century, China in particular became the global leader in steel production, a status it continues to hold today. In fact, China now produces more than half of the world’s steel, shaping not only global markets but also geopolitics.

Varieties, Strengths, and Weaknesses of Steel

One of steel’s most remarkable qualities is its versatility. There is not just one kind of steel but thousands of varieties, each engineered to balance properties such as hardness, ductility, resistance to corrosion, or tensile strength. Carbon steels, which are the most common, range from low-carbon steels used in construction to high-carbon steels used in cutting tools and springs. Stainless steel, first developed in the early 20th century, resists rust and corrosion, making it essential in everything from kitchenware to surgical instruments. Alloy steels incorporate metals such as nickel, chromium, or vanadium, creating specialized materials used in aerospace or high-performance machinery.

Despite its strengths, steel is not without limitations. It is heavy compared to materials such as aluminum or titanium, which restricts its use in certain applications where weight is critical. Steel also corrodes if unprotected, especially in harsh environments, and while stainless steels solve part of this problem, they come at a higher cost. Furthermore, the process of making steel is energy-intensive, which has long been both a technical and economic challenge. These strengths and weaknesses ensure that while steel dominates most industries, it is constantly balanced against other materials in engineering decisions.

Steel in the World’s Great Projects

Some of the greatest engineering achievements in history were made possible only because of steel. The Golden Gate Bridge in San Francisco, completed in 1937, is one of the most iconic examples. Its construction required more than 80,000 tons of steel, and its two main cables contain enough wire to circle the Earth more than three times. The bridge’s striking International Orange towers, rising 746 feet above the water, would not have been possible without the strength and flexibility of steel cables, which allowed the span to stretch an unprecedented 4,200 feet across the mouth of the bay.

Skyscrapers, too, owe their existence to steel. The Empire State Building in New York, completed in 1931, used more than 57,000 tons of structural steel, which was erected at a breathtaking pace of nearly four and a half stories per week. Without steel’s strength and fire resistance, the vertical cities of the modern era would have remained dreams on the drafting table.

In the 21st century, megaprojects continue to push the limits of steel. The Burj Khalifa in Dubai, the tallest building in the world, incorporates a steel-reinforced structure that allows it to soar more than half a mile into the sky. Similarly, modern suspension bridges such as Japan’s Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, with its record-breaking main span of nearly 2,000 meters, would be unthinkable without specialized high-strength steels capable of withstanding immense tension. These feats illustrate not only steel’s unmatched strength but also its role in shaping the human imagination, allowing engineers to design structures that redefine what is possible.

Research and the Future of Steel

While steel is ancient in its origins, it is still very much a subject of cutting-edge research. Modern materials science has opened new frontiers in steel design, including nanostructured steels engineered at the atomic level to achieve combinations of strength and toughness once thought impossible. These advanced steels are lighter, stronger, and more resistant to wear, making them ideal for aerospace, energy, and medical applications.

Production methods are also evolving. Traditional blast furnaces remain dominant, but they rely heavily on coal, which contributes significantly to global emissions. New approaches, such as hydrogen-based reduction, are being explored to reduce reliance on carbon fuels while maintaining efficiency. Electric arc furnaces, which melt down scrap steel using electricity, are also gaining prominence, making it possible to recycle steel endlessly without losing its properties. These advances address one of the material’s enduring challenges: how to produce it more efficiently and sustainably without compromising performance.

As researchers continue to explore new alloys, refine microstructures, and develop cleaner production techniques, steel’s story remains unfinished. It is a material that has carried humanity from the blacksmith’s forge to the tallest skyscrapers, and it continues to evolve as scientists and engineers seek to unlock its next chapter. From monumental bridges to nanostructured alloys, steel is both a material of history and a material of the future, a foundation upon which civilization itself has been built and will continue to grow.
